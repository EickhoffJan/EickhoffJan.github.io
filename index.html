Academic Theses

The Jung Method for Resolving Surface Singularities
Bachelor's Thesis, TUM • Supervised by Prof. Dr. Christian Liedtke
+
This thesis presents Jung’s method for the resolution of surface singularities in characteristic 0. Starting from a finite projection to the projective plane, it analyzes the associated branch curve, applies embedded resolution, and explains how the problem reduces to resolving cyclic quotient singularities.

On the Noetherian Property of Rings as Modules over Larger Rings
April 24, 2025

Question

Let \( R \subseteq S \) be rings. Then, if \( R \) is Noetherian as an \( S \)-module, is \( R \) also Noetherian as an \( R \)-module?

At first glance one might expect “yes,” but the following counterexample shows that, in complete generality, the answer is no.

Counterexample

Let \(K\) be a field,

\[ R = K[x_1,x_2,\dots], \quad S = \text{End}_K(R), \]

and embed \(R\) into \(S\) via

\[ \iota\colon R \; \hookrightarrow \; S, \quad r \;\mapsto\; \phi_r, \]

where \(\phi_r\) is the endomorphism \(x\mapsto r\,x\). We view \(R\) as an \(S\)-module by

\[ f\cdot x \;=\; f(x), \quad f\in S,\ x\in R. \]

Note that this is not the usual multiplication in \(R\).

Let \(x \in R\) be nonzero. Then \[ S\cdot x = \{\,f(x)\mid f\in S\}\subseteq R. \] Given any \(y\in R\), extend \(x\) to a \(K\)-basis \(B\) of the vector space \(R\). Define a \(K\)-linear map \[ f\colon R\to R,\quad f(x)=y,\quad f(b)=0\ \text{for }b\in B\setminus\{x\}. \] This map is well-defined, linear, and extends uniquely to \( R \). Hence \(f\in S\) and \(f(x)=y\). Hence \(S\cdot x=R\).

Thus every nonzero element generates the entire \(S\)-module, so \(R\) is simple – and hence Noetherian – as an \(S\)-module. On the other hand, \(R=K[x_1,x_2,\dots]\) is not Noetherian as a ring, and therefore not Noetherian as an \(R\)-module.

The General Factorization Case

We now impose that the \(S\)-action on \(R\) factors through \(R\) itself.

Theorem (General Factorization)

Let \( R \subseteq S \) be rings, and suppose that \( R \) carries the structure of an \( S \)-module, where the \( S \)-action factors through \( R \). Then every \( R \)-submodule of \( R \) is already an \( S \)-submodule.

Proof.

Consider the ring homomorphism \( \varphi : S \to R \) through which the \( S \)-action on \( R \) is defined. Accordingly, for all \( s \in S \) and \( r \in R \), we have \[ s \cdot r = \varphi(s) \cdot r. \] Let \( M \subseteq R \) be an \( R \)-submodule. Then the \( S \)-action on \( M \) satisfies \[ S \cdot M = \varphi(S) \cdot M \subseteq R \cdot M \subseteq M. \] Hence, \( M \) is closed under the \( S \)-action and thus constitutes an \( S \)-submodule of \( R \).

Corollary

Under the hypotheses of the General Factorization Theorem, if \(R\) is Noetherian as an \(S\)-module, then \(R\) is Noetherian as an \(R\)-module.

The Surjective Case

Next, we consider the situation where \(R\) is a quotient of \(S\).

Theorem (Surjective Case)

Let \( R \subseteq S \) be rings, and let \( I \subseteq S \) be an ideal such that \[ R \cong S/I. \] In particular, \( R \) acquires the natural structure of an \( S \)-module. Under this identification, the \( S \)-submodules and \( R \)-submodules of \( R \) coincide.

Proof.

Consider the canonical surjective ring homomorphism \( \pi : S \to S/I \) through which the action of \( S \) on \( R \) is defined. Then, for all \( s \in S \) and \( r \in R \), the following holds: \[ s \cdot r = \pi(s) \cdot r. \] Let \( N \subseteq R \) be an \( S \)-submodule of \( R \). Then, \[ R \cdot N = \pi(S) \cdot N = S \cdot N \subseteq N. \] Thus, \( N \) is stable under the \( R \)-action, implying that \( N \) is also an \( R \)-submodule of \( R \). In view of the General Factorization Theorem, it follows that the \( S \)- and \( R \)-submodule structures on \( R \) are identical.

Corollary

Under the hypotheses of the Surjective Case, \(R\) is Noetherian as an \(S\)-module if and only if \(R\) is Noetherian as an \(R\)-module.

Example

Let \(R\) be any ring. Consider the evaluation homomorphism

\[ \varphi\colon R[x]\to R,\quad f(x)\mapsto f(0). \]

Then \(R\) becomes an \(R[x]\)-module by \(f\cdot r = f(0)\,r\). Since \(R\cong R[x]/(x)\), this falls under the Surjective Case, and one sees immediately that \(R\) is Noetherian as an \(R[x]\)-module exactly when it is Noetherian as a ring.
